# The Hollowed Keep — World Bible

> Every door demands a toll. The Keep's toll is you.

This is the master story document. Everything in the game should trace back to something here. Edit freely — this is a living document.

---

## 1. The Premise

The Hollowed Keep surfaces where it pleases. One moonless night it stands on the moor where no building stood before, gates open, windows dark, patient. It does not advertise. It doesn't need to. The desperate find it: debtors, the dying, the disgraced, treasure-hunters, people with nothing left to lose — which is the Keep's favorite kind of wrong.

Crossing the threshold costs you your *self*. Your name, your past, your trade, the faces you loved, the reason you came. This is the Toll, and it is not negotiable, and most who pay it don't know they're paying until they wake on the flagstones inside the gate with empty hands and an empty everything else.

You keep three things: your body (blood remembers what the mind cannot — your race and its gifts survive), your language, and one **Remnant** — a single fragment the Keep failed to take, or chose to leave. A locket. A scar. A melody stuck in your head. It is your only clue to who you were.

You are not building a character. You are **refilling one**.

## 2. What the Keep Is

The Keep is the first hollowed thing.

Something scooped it out long ago — what, and why, is the deepest mystery in the game and stays a whisper until very late. Ever since, the Keep has been filling itself: taking selves, taking treasures, taking whole *places*, and never once feeling full. It is not evil the way a villain is evil. It is hungry the way a well is deep.

What the Keep takes doesn't vanish. It **settles**. Selves, heirlooms, villages, battlefields — everything it swallows sinks slowly through its body like sediment, forming floors. The deeper you descend, the older the Takings. At the very bottom lies the **Vault of Names**: the hoard of every self the Keep has ever taken. Including yours.

The player and the Keep mirror each other. Both are hollow. Both are filling themselves — the Keep by *taking*, the player by *doing*. That's the thematic spine of the whole game: **what fills a hollow — what you take, or what you make?** Every major mechanic (skills carved by deeds, tolls paid at stairs, the final choice) should rhyme with this question.

## 3. The Voice of the Keep

The Keep watches, and the Keep comments. It is the game's system voice — the DCC-style personality layer, skinned native to our premise.

The voice is: **old, dry, patient, faintly amused, occasionally hungry.** It has eaten ten thousand adventurers and found maybe six of them interesting. It is never zany, never cruel for cruelty's sake, and it never winks at the camera. It finds mortals briefly fascinating the way you might find an ant carrying something unusually large.

Where the voice appears:

- **System messages**, bracketed: `[ The Keep takes notice. ]`
- **Skill discoveries** ("carvings"): `[ Skill carved: DEMOLITIONS I — You made fire your argument. The Keep finds you persuasive. ]`
- **Engravings** (achievements): permanent marks with dry flavor text. *"FIRST BLOOD — Something died and it wasn't you. Statistically remarkable."*
- **Class offers**: formal proposals. The Keep *proposes*; it never assigns.
- **Death**: `[ The Keep accepts your offering. ]` — followed by an itemized note of what it took.

Room descriptions stay atmospheric and "objective" (the prototype's gothic register is good — keep that quality). The wry voice lives in the bracketed system layer and the Engravings, so horror and humor never dilute each other. NPCs carry the warmth.

## 4. Structure: The Descent

The general goal is known almost immediately and takes the whole game to reach: **everything the Keep ever took is hoarded at the bottom. Descend far enough and you can take yours back — if by then you still want it.**

### The Floors

Floors are strata of things the Keep has swallowed. Oldest lie deepest. Each floor has its own biome, factions, dangers, skills that shine, and a **Warden** — a boss, usually built from the strongest self the Keep ever took in that place.

| # | Floor | What it is |
|---|-------|-----------|
| 0 | **The Threshold** | Gate, courtyard, outbuildings. You wake hollow. Small, near-safe, learn-by-doing. (~10 rooms) |
| 1 | **The Keep Proper** | The castle itself — halls, library, chapel, towers. The "tutorial that isn't": open exploration, skill discoveries everywhere, first class offers at the end. (Rework of the prototype's 41 rooms.) |
| 2 | **The Undercroft** | Dungeons, cells, catacombs. The Keep's digestion begins. (Expands the prototype's Deep.) |
| 3 | **The Drowned Village** | The first Taking: a fishing village swallowed whole, still dripping. Its people still go through the motions of a market day, underwater-pale. |
| 4 | **The Library of Ash** | Every burned book, every unfinished manuscript, every dead language the Keep could catch. Knowledge floor — dangerous to *read*. |
| 5 | **The Battlefield Eternal** | A war the Keep took mid-battle. Both armies still fighting, centuries in. Neither remembers why. |
| 6 | **The Stolen Cathedral** | A holy site taken with its god still in it. Or something claiming to be. |
| 7 | **The Orchard of Hands** | A taken forest, wrong now. Growth floor — things the Keep planted from what it ate. |
| 8 | **The Foundry** | Where the Keep *makes*: Wardens, walls, its own repairs. Industrial nightmare. The Masons' origin. |
| 9 | **The Hollow Court** | The selves the Keep found too interesting to shelve — kept awake, a court of brilliant, broken figures who have been playing politics for a thousand years. |
| 10 | **The Vault of Names** | The hoard. The heart. The ending. |

Ten floors is the design skeleton, built over time — Floors 0–2 first. Side-veins (**Oubliettes**, optional pocket dungeons off any floor) give breadth without blocking the spine.

### Scale & Density

This game is BIG. Targets, honest about phasing:

- **Room counts:** Floor 0 ~12 rooms. Floor 1: 60–80 (the prototype's 41, rebuilt and grown). Every floor below: 50–100+. Oubliettes: 8–20 rooms each, 2–3 per floor. Full-spine target: **600–800 rooms** plus side content.
- **Time target:** the spine alone is a long-weekend-minimum for a focused player. Completionists (every hearth, every hidden skill, every NPC thread, every Oubliette) should be looking at something much longer. Nothing about this game should be finishable in an afternoon.
- **Density rule:** every room earns its place — a secret, a solution-ingredient, a lore fragment, a skill opportunity, or a really good sentence. No corridor filler. If a floor needs to ship smaller and denser first and grow later, that's the right call. Polish over acreage.
- **The infinite-adventurers feel:** the Keep must feel like it has been eating delvers forever. Instruments: the Rune Wall and Chronicle (already built), corpses and journals of prior delvers (loot + story + warnings), NPCs who misremember you as delvers from decades ago, Engravings referencing the failures of people who came before, classes you unlock by finding *practitioners* — living, dead, or worse. Depth of time is cheaper than breadth of map and just as convincing.

### The Stairs Between

Descending is never free. Every stair demands a toll — smaller than the gate's, but *chosen*. The Stairs offer terms: an item of worth, a measure of blood (max HP until next hearth), a truth spoken aloud, a memory of the floor above (fogs your map of it). What you pay is a roleplay choice with real consequences, and it keeps the founding premise mechanically alive all game long.

## 5. People of the Keep

Everyone inside is hollowed. What varies is what they've refilled themselves with.

**Wick** — a lantern-bearer so old it has forgotten how to be a person; now mostly a helpful shape holding a lamp. Early-game guide and comic-melancholy heart. Speaks in borrowed phrases from selves the Keep digested — sometimes uselessly, sometimes with devastating accuracy.

**The Porter** — takes the Toll at the gate. Faintly apologetic. "Terms are terms." First voice you hear; recurring presence at strange moments.

**The Remembrancer** — catalogs what the Keep takes. Can find, and sometimes *sell back*, small memories. May be quietly stealing from the Vault. Central quest-giver for the Remnant/past thread.

**The Hollow Market** — hub where hollowed souls trade. Bartholomew the ghost merchant migrates here from the prototype, and the Talking Skull absolutely survives — both fit the new premise without changing a word of their personalities.

**The wider roster** (each ships with a personal questline when they ship — see the NPC design rule below):

- **Mother Lye** — Unburdened priestess. Serene, kind, quietly terrifying. Wants to help you stop wanting your past back.
- **Foreman Grist** — Mason overseer, permanently exhausted. Hires delvers for repair runs into places the Keep is "digesting wrong."
- **Sera Vail** — Reclaimer captain. Charismatic, driven, keeps a list of every name her company has recovered. The list has a problem she hasn't noticed yet.
- **Hesk the Rat-Catcher** — hunts the Keep's vermin, or claims to. The rats seem weirdly fine with him. (Gateway to the King of Rats thread.)
- **The Anchorite** — walled themselves INTO the Keep voluntarily, pre-hollowing, and speaks through a slot in the stone. Knows more lore than anyone. Every answer costs a candle.
- **Twinless Tam** — one of two twins. The Keep took the other at the gate. Unless it took Tam, and the one at the gate walked free. Tam isn't sure either.
- **The Collector of Small Things** — buys oddities nobody else values: buttons, baby teeth, final words. Pays surprisingly well. Do not ask what the collection is *for*.

**NPC design rule:** every named NPC ships with (1) a personal questline of 2–5 steps unique to them, (2) a favor track — help them and it's remembered, (3) a betrayal branch — you can rob them, sell them out, or worse, and the world reacts (reputation, prices, locked doors, occasionally a knife), and (4) their true Name findable in the Vault of Names if you've earned max favor — a late-game reunion, and a choice about whether to give it back. NPCs release in waves with their floors; each one polished before the next wave starts.

**Factions** (join, aid, or cross):

- **The Reclaimers** — descend to take their selves back. Militant hope. The "default" path — until you learn what reclaiming costs.
- **The Unburdened** — a cult that holds hollow as *holy*. The past was weight; the Keep is mercy. Unsettling because they seem genuinely at peace.
- **The Masons** — repair the Keep. Traitors? No: if the Keep dies, everything it holds dissolves — every floor, every soul in it. They keep the cage standing because the cage is the world now. The game's best moral argument.

## 6. Endings (sketch — refine together before Floor 9 is built)

At the Vault, the Keep assembles *who you were* — from your Remnant, and from how you actually played. Sneaks discover they were a spy; brawlers, a soldier; readers, a scholar who came here on purpose. Then the choice:

1. **Reclaim** — take your old self back and leave. But the self you built in the Keep dissolves; you'll remember it the way you remember a dream.
2. **Refuse** — you like who you made better. Walk back up as the only person ever to leave the Keep *by choice*, or claim a place in it.
3. **The Gift** — give the Keep your whole self, old and built, freely. The only willingly-given self it has ever held. Something breaks open. The gates open — everyone's gates.
4. **The Hollow Throne** *(hidden)* — the Keep is a vessel, and vessels can change hands. Take it. Become the hunger.

## 7. Tone Guide

Gothic + wry, DCC-energy, never a ripoff. Rules of thumb:

- Horror lives in details and implications, not gore. (The prototype's straw doll on a pile of bones is the gold standard. More of that.)
- The Keep's humor is dry observation, never jokes. It doesn't punch down at the player; it *studies* them.
- NPCs are the warmth. The world is cold; the people (and skulls) in it are not.
- Absurdity is allowed when it's earned by player action (surviving three falls unlocks *Falling (With Style)*), never by random wackiness.
- Presentation stays CRT-terminal, ASCII art, old-internet MUD. The aesthetic is a promise: someone made this because they thought it was cool.

## 8. Open Questions (to settle as we build)

- What hollowed the Keep? (Deepest lore. Settled: we decide this together later, before Floor 8 is built; breadcrumbs start appearing from Floor 4 on.)
- NPC selves in the Vault: **settled — yes**, gated behind prerequisites (max favor on that NPC's track, at minimum). Folded into the NPC design rule in §5. The Wick reunion is the flagship version.
- Multiplayer-era: other players are simply other hollowed souls — the chronicle/rune wall already fits. Design floors so nothing breaks when a second player exists.
